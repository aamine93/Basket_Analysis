import networkx as nx

# Create a directed graph
G = nx.DiGraph()

# Add edges for the top 10 rules

for _, row in top10.iterrows():
    antecedents = row['antecedents'].split(", ")
    consequent = row['consequents']
    for item in antecedents:
        G.add_edge(item, consequent, weight=row['lift'], confidence=row['confidence'])

# Draw the graph

)

Market Basket Analysis Report¶

We analyzed grocery shopping data to understand which products customers tend to buy together. The goal was to turn raw transactional data into clear insights that could support promotions, product placement, and bundling strategies. By using data mining techniques, we were able to identify meaningful product combinations that repeat across many transactions. These findings can help retailers increase sales and improve the shopping experience.

Data Preparation and Approach¶

We started by cleaning the dataset and transforming it into a format suitable for analysis. Each row in the original dataset represented a transaction, and we converted this data into a basket matrix where each customer was represented as a row and each product was represented as a column. If a customer bought an item, it was marked with a 1; otherwise, it was marked with a 0.

This binary format allowed us to clearly see purchase patterns across all customers. We then applied the Apriori algorithm, a well-known method for market basket analysis, to extract frequent product combinations. From there, we generated association rules that helped us understand how products are related.

Key Metrics for Analysis¶

To ensure our results were meaningful, we relied on three main metrics:

  • Support measures how frequently a combination of products appears in all transactions. This tells us if a product pairing is common enough to be worth considering.
  • Confidence represents the likelihood that if a customer buys one product, they will also buy another. This is expressed as a percentage.
  • Lift shows whether two products are purchased together more often than would be expected by chance. A lift greater than 1 means there is a strong positive relationship between the products.

We filtered our results to only keep the most significant rules. Specifically, we selected rules where the lift was greater than 1.2, the confidence was greater than 40%, and the support was above 2%.

This filtering is correct because:

  • Lift > 1.2 ensures we only keep product pairs that are 20% stronger than random chance.
  • Confidence > 40% means the relationship is reliable enough to act on.
  • Support > 2% removes rare rules that would have little business impact.
  • We then sorted the rules by lift to prioritize the strongest patterns first.

This approach ensures we are not looking at random or trivial relationships, but instead at product combinations that actually matter in real-world shopping behavior.

Key Findings from the Data¶

After applying these filters, we identified clear product relationships. Here are the five most important rules:

  • If customers buy [rolls/buns, sausage, whole milk], they also buy [yogurt] with 46.8% confidence and lift 1.66.
  • If customers buy [other vegetables, sausage, whole milk], they also buy [yogurt] with 45.9% confidence and lift 1.62.
  • If customers buy [other vegetables, root vegetables, whole milk], they also buy [yogurt] with 44.1% confidence and lift 1.56.
  • If customers buy [rolls/buns, sausage], they also buy [yogurt] with 43.3% confidence and lift 1.53.
  • If customers buy [sausage, tropical fruit], they also buy [yogurt] with 43.2% confidence and lift 1.53.

These findings show that yogurt consistently appears in multiple high-confidence rules. This means that yogurt is strongly connected to several different product combinations. Sausage, bread, and milk are also important drivers of these relationships.

What this tells us is simple: yogurt is not just an isolated purchase; it is a complementary product that is often added when certain core items are in the basket.

Visual Analysis of the Results¶

To make the results clearer, we used several visualizations to explain the patterns:

Support vs. Confidence Scatter Plot¶

This chart compares how often product rules occur (support) with how reliable they are (confidence). Rules in the upper-right section of this plot are the strongest because they are both frequent and highly reliable. This helps confirm that our filtered rules are not just rare coincidences but real, meaningful patterns.

Lift vs. Confidence Scatter Plot¶

This visualization compares how strong product relationships are (lift) with their confidence. Strong rules stand out with higher lift values. In our case, the rules involving yogurt consistently appear as high-lift, reinforcing its role as a complementary product.

Top 10 Rules Bar Chart¶

We also created a bar chart ranking the top 10 rules by their lift value. This makes it clear that yogurt dominates several of the highest-ranked combinations. This chart provides a straightforward way to prioritize which rules should be acted upon.

Network Graph¶

Finally, we built a network graph to visually map product relationships. Each product is represented as a node, and arrows show how certain products lead to others. Yogurt is at the center of this graph, connected to multiple items like sausage, rolls/buns, and milk. This visualization makes it very clear which items are driving complementary purchases.

What These Results Mean¶

These insights can be applied directly to business decisions. For example:

  • Cross-selling: If a customer adds sausage or bread to their basket, yogurt can be recommended.
  • Product placement: Yogurt can be placed near sausage, bread, or milk in stores to encourage impulse purchases.
  • Bundling offers: Promotions such as "Buy sausage and bread, get a discount on yogurt" could increase overall basket size.
  • Targeted marketing: Online ads or coupons can be focused on these combinations to maximize effectiveness.

By using these data-driven insights, businesses can move away from guessing and instead make decisions that match how customers actually shop.

Conclusion¶

This analysis shows how transaction data can be turned into clear, actionable strategies. Yogurt stands out as a product with strong cross-selling potential, especially when paired with sausage, bread, and milk. The metrics and visualizations we used confirm that these are not random patterns but meaningful behaviors that can drive sales growth.

By leveraging these findings, a retailer can improve promotions, create smarter store layouts, and develop marketing campaigns that feel natural to customers because they reflect how people already shop.
